Unit I · Topic 01

Introduction to the Thorax & Thoracic Cavity

Source deck: “Introduction to Thorax and Thoracic Cavity” — Slides 1–17

Overview

The thorax is the upper portion of the trunk, running from the base of the neck down to the abdomen. The diaphragm splits the trunk cavity into two: the thoracic cavity above and the abdominal cavity below. Inside the thoracic cavity sit the lungs — the main organs of breathing — which are held apart by a thick, mobile partition called the mediastinum. The heart and the great vessels are the chief occupants of the mediastinum.Slide 3

The Thoracic Cage

A bony-and-cartilaginous framework, the thoracic cage, supports the thorax. It anchors muscles of the chest, upper limb, back and diaphragm, and because it is part bone and part cartilage it retains some elasticity. Its main job is mechanical: by expanding and contracting it raises or lowers the pressure inside the chest, drawing air into the lungs on inspiration and pushing it out on expiration.Slide 4

Three components build the cage: the sternum in front, twelve thoracic vertebrae (with their discs) behind, and twelve pairs of ribs with their costal cartilages forming the sides.Slide 5

The Rib Cage — How the Ribs Attach

  • Every rib articulates with the vertebral column posteriorly.
  • Ribs 1–7 attach directly to the side of the sternum via their own costal cartilage.
  • Ribs 8–10 attach indirectly — their cartilages join the cartilage of the rib above rather than the sternum itself.
  • Ribs 11–12 have free-floating cartilage tips and make no anterior connection at all.Slide 6

Superior Thoracic Aperture (Thoracic Inlet)

The thoracic cavity opens into the root of the neck through a narrow gap, the thoracic inlet, bounded by the top edge of the manubrium in front, the front edge of the T1 vertebral body behind, and the medial edges of the first ribs and cartilages on each side. Because the first rib slants downward and forward, the front of this boundary sits roughly 1.5 inches lower than the back of it; the manubrium's upper edge lines up with the top of T3.Slides 7–8

Suprapleural Membrane (Sibson's Fascia)

A tent-shaped fibrous sheet, Sibson's fascia, seals each side of the thoracic inlet. Passing through the inlet are the sternohyoid and sternothyroid muscles; the brachiocephalic trunk, left common carotid and left subclavian arteries; the vagus nerves, left recurrent laryngeal nerve and phrenic nerves; the brachiocephalic and inferior thyroid veins; and the oesophagus, trachea and lung apices.Slide 9

Inferior Thoracic Aperture (Thoracic Outlet)

This wide opening surrounds the top of the abdominal cavity and is bridged by the diaphragm, which therefore separates thorax from abdomen. Its boundaries are the xiphisternal joint anteriorly, the body of T12 posteriorly, and the costal margin with ribs 11–12 on each side.Slide 10

The Diaphragm

A large, dome-shaped musculo-tendinous sheet closes the thoracic outlet and, because it separates the two great cavities, is sometimes called the thoraco-abdominal diaphragm. It is the principal muscle of breathing and consists of a peripheral muscular rim plus a central fibrous sheet, the central tendon.Slide 11

Neurovascular Supply

Motor nerve supply: right and left phrenic nerves, assisted sensorily by the lower five intercostal and subcostal nerves. Arteries: superior and inferior phrenic, pericardiophrenic, musculophrenic, superior epigastric, and the lower five intercostal/subcostal arteries. Lymphatics: anterior, posterior, right-lateral and left-lateral diaphragmatic node groups.Slide 14

Functions of the Diaphragm

  • Chief muscle of inspiration — contraction flattens the dome and enlarges the thoracic cavity vertically.
  • Weight-lifting aid — a deep breath held against a closed glottis raises intra-abdominal pressure, stabilising the vertebral column and assisting the back muscles when lifting.
  • Thoraco-abdominal pump — descent of the dome lowers thoracic pressure while raising abdominal pressure, squeezing the inferior vena cava and pushing venous blood up toward the right atrium.
  • Oesophageal sphincter action — fibres of the right crus wrap the oesophageal opening and contribute a sphincter-like squeeze.

Slide 15

Clinical Correlations

Diaphragmatic paralysis: injury to one phrenic nerve paralyses that half of the diaphragm; bilateral phrenic nerve injury paralyses the whole diaphragm and can precipitate respiratory failure.

Hiccups: involuntary, spasmodic diaphragmatic contractions paired with sudden glottic closure. Commonly triggered by gastric irritation after eating or drinking; pathological causes include irritation of the diaphragm or phrenic nerve, hysteria, and uraemia.

Bones & Joints of the Thorax

Source deck: “Bones and Joints of the Thorax” — Slides 1–21

The bony framework of the thorax protects the thoracic viscera while remaining dynamic — its joints allow the continual movement needed for breathing. Three bone groups build it: the sternum, twelve pairs of ribs, and twelve thoracic vertebrae.Slide 2

The Sternum

A flat, elongated bone roughly 7 cm long lying in the anterior midline of the chest wall, broad and thick above, thin and pointed below. It has a slightly rough, convex front surface and a smooth, gently concave back surface, and comprises three parts: the manubrium, the body, and the xiphoid process.Slide 3

Manubrium

A roughly quadrilateral, thick, strong plate lying opposite the T3–T4 vertebral level. Its front surface gives attachment on each side to the sternal heads of sternocleidomastoid and pectoralis major, while its smooth back surface forms the front wall of the superior mediastinum. Sternohyoid attaches near the clavicular notch and sternothyroid near the facet for the first costal cartilage. The thick, rounded, concave upper border carries the suprasternal (jugular) notch, flanked by clavicular notches that form the sternoclavicular joints. The lower border meets the body of the sternum at the manubriosternal joint (a secondary cartilaginous joint), producing a palpable kink called the sternal angle (angle of Louis).Slides 4–5

Body of the Sternum

Longer, narrower and thinner than the manubrium, and broadest at its lower end. It joins the manubrium above (manubriosternal joint) and the xiphoid process below (a primary cartilaginous xiphisternal joint). Its lateral edges articulate with the 2nd–7th costal cartilages via synovial joints — technically the 2nd cartilage meets the sternum right at the manubriosternal junction and the 7th right at the xiphisternal junction.Slide 6

Xiphoid Process

The smallest, lowest part of the sternum, quite variable in shape — sometimes bifid, sometimes perforated. Its front face gives insertion to the medial fibres of rectus abdominis, its back face gives origin to sternal fibres of the diaphragm, and its tip anchors the upper end of the linea alba.Slide 7

Why the Sternal Angle Matters

Palpable as a transverse ridge about 5 cm below the jugular notch, the sternal angle is a key surface landmark: the 2nd costal cartilage meets the sternum here (a reference point for counting ribs); it sits opposite the T4/T5 disc; a horizontal plane through it divides superior from inferior mediastinum; the ascending aorta ends and the aortic arch begins and ends at this level; the descending aorta begins here; the trachea bifurcates here; the pulmonary trunk splits into its two branches here; the upper border of the heart lies here; and the azygos vein arches over the right lung root to enter the SVC at this level.Slide 8

Ossification

The sternum develops from paired vertical cartilage plates that fuse in the midline, ossifying from six separate centres — one for the manubrium (5th month), four for the body (6th–9th month), and one for the xiphoid (3rd year). Fusion of the plates proceeds bottom-to-top, starting at puberty and finishing by age 25; the xiphoid fuses to the body around age 40; the manubrium, however, typically never fuses to the body, so the manubriosternal joint usually persists for life.Slide 9

Clinical Correlations

Sternal puncture (bone-marrow aspiration): the manubrium is favoured because it is subcutaneous and easy to access; a thick needle is inserted into its upper part to avoid the aortic arch lying behind the lower manubrium. It is avoided in children, whose thin sternal plates make it easy to drive the needle straight through into the arch of the aorta, risking fatal haemorrhage.

Sternal fracture: typically follows chest trauma against a steering wheel in road accidents and classically fractures at the sternal angle; backward displacement of the fragments can injure the aorta, heart or liver and cause severe, potentially fatal bleeding.

Slide 10

Thoracic Vertebrae

Twelve vertebrae forming the posterior wall of the thoracic cage and articulating with the ribs to permit the movements of respiration. Each has a body, vertebral arch, spinous process, transverse processes, articular processes and vertebral foramen.Slide 11

  • Body: heart-shaped, intermediate in size between cervical and lumbar vertebrae, and studded with costal facets for the rib heads. T1–T10 carry two facet pairs (superior and inferior); T11–T12 carry only one pair, since they meet the floating ribs.Slide 12
  • Vertebral arch: encircles and protects the spinal cord, built from short, thick pedicles and flat laminae that close the ring posteriorly.Slide 13
  • Spinous processes: long, sloping downward and overlapping — this arrangement shields the cord and limits the range of thoracic spinal motion, adding stability.Slide 14
  • Transverse processes: large, club-shaped, projecting laterally and slightly backward; a facet near the tip meets the tubercle of the correspondingly numbered rib. The superior articular facets face backward and slightly laterally to meet the vertebra above; the inferior facets face forward, downward and medially to meet the vertebra below.Slide 15

The Ribs

Twelve curved bone pairs forming the protective lattice of the thoracic cavity, articulating with the vertebrae and shielding the heart and lungs. They are classified by how (or whether) they reach the sternum.Slide 16

Costal Cartilages

Hyaline cartilage bars that give the chest wall elasticity and mobility. The 1st–7th cartilages connect their ribs to the sternum and lengthen progressively from 1 to 7. The 8th–10th cartilages attach to the cartilage above them, shortening progressively. The 11th and 12th end freely as pointed tips.Slide 18

Clinical Correlations
  • Sternal puncture / bone-marrow biopsy: exploits the manubrium's superficial position and haematopoietic marrow to diagnose blood disorders such as leukaemia or anaemia.
  • Sternal fractures: usually from direct trauma (e.g. seatbelt injury in a collision); may injure the heart or great vessels lying behind.
  • Median sternotomy: a surgical midline split of the sternum used to access the heart and great vessels, as in open-heart surgery.

The Thoracic Wall

Source deck: “Thoracic Wall” — Slides 1–24

The thoracic wall is built posteriorly by the thoracic vertebral column, anteriorly by the sternum and costal cartilages, and laterally by the ribs and the spaces between them. Its floor is the diaphragm and its roof is formed by the suprapleural membranes.Slide 3

Surface Lines of Orientation

Clinicians describe locations on the chest wall using a standard set of vertical reference lines: the midsternal line down the sternum's midline; the midclavicular line from the clavicle's midpoint to the mid-inguinal point; the anterior axillary line from the anterior axillary fold; the midaxillary line midway between the anterior and posterior folds; the posterior axillary line from the posterior fold; and the scapular line through the inferior angle of the scapula (arms at the sides).Slides 4–5

Coverings, Superficial to Deep

Skin → superficial fascia → deep fascia → muscle.Slide 6

Intercostal Spaces

There are eleven intercostal spaces, one between each pair of adjacent ribs. Each contains intercostal muscles, an intercostal nerve, intercostal artery and vein, and intercostal lymphatics.Slide 7

Muscles of the Thoracic Cage

Five muscles reshape the thoracic cavity's volume during breathing: external intercostal, internal intercostal, innermost intercostal, subcostal, and transversus thoracis.Slide 8

Levator Costarum (×12)

Positioned on either side of the posterior thorax lateral to the vertebral column, arising from the transverse process tips of C7–T11 and running obliquely down and lateral to insert between the tubercle and angle of the rib below. It elevates and rotates the rib neck and also rotates/laterally flexes the vertebral column.Slide 14

Intercostal Nerves

Twelve pairs of spinal nerves split into anterior and posterior rami on leaving the intervertebral foramina. The anterior rami of T1–T11 travel through the intercostal spaces as the intercostal nerves proper; the T12 anterior ramus runs below the 12th rib as the subcostal nerve. Posterior rami pass backward beside the vertebral articular processes to supply the vertebral joints, deep back muscles and overlying skin.Slide 15

Nerves 3, 4, 5 and 6 are considered typical, staying confined to their own intercostal space; nerves 1, 2 and 7–11 are atypical, extending beyond the thoracic wall into neighbouring regions.Slide 16

Intercostal Vessels

Each intercostal space carries one posterior intercostal artery and two anterior intercostal arteries (an upper and lower branch). Within the space, the posterior intercostal artery runs between the vein above it and the nerve below it — the classic "VAN" order — and terminates by joining the upper anterior intercostal artery of that space.Slides 17–18

Anterior intercostal arteries for spaces 1–6 branch from the internal thoracic artery; those for spaces 7 and 9 branch from the musculophrenic artery; spaces 10 and 11 have no anterior intercostal artery at all, as they don't reach far enough forward.Slide 19

Anterior intercostal veins exist only in the upper nine spaces (two per space, paralleling the arteries); veins 1–6 drain into the internal thoracic vein and veins 7–9 into the musculophrenic vein. Each space has a single posterior intercostal vein that parallels its artery.Slides 20–21

Intercostal Lymphatics

Anterior parts of each space drain to anterior intercostal (internal mammary) nodes; posterior parts drain to posterior intercostal nodes. Efferents from the posterior nodes of the lower four spaces merge into a trunk descending to the cisterna chyli; efferents from the upper spaces' posterior nodes drain into the right lymphatic duct (right side) or thoracic duct (left side). Posterior intercostal nodes sit near the rib necks posteriorly; anterior intercostal nodes lie along the internal thoracic (mammary) artery.Slides 22–23

Clinical Correlations
  • Paracentesis thoracis (pleural fluid aspiration): the needle must never enter medial to the rib angle, since the posterior intercostal artery crosses the space obliquely there.
  • Intercostal nerve block: local anaesthetic injected between the internal and innermost intercostal muscles.
  • Intercostal muscle paralysis can follow intercostal nerve injury.
  • Thoracotomy incisions are placed just above the upper border of the rib below to avoid the neurovascular bundle running along the rib above.

The Mediastinum

Source deck: “Mediastinum” — Slides 1–20

The mediastinum is the central partition of the thoracic cavity lying between the two pleural sacs. It holds every thoracic structure except the lungs themselves, and because its contents are hollow viscera joined loosely by connective tissue, it is quite mobile. It stretches from the superior thoracic aperture down to the diaphragm, and from the sternum/costal cartilages in front to the vertebral bodies behind.Slide 2

Boundaries: sternum (anterior), vertebral column (posterior), superior thoracic aperture (superior), diaphragm (inferior), mediastinal pleura (lateral).Slide 3

How the Mediastinum Is Divided

A transverse plane through the sternal angle (anteriorly) and the T4/T5 disc (posteriorly) splits the mediastinum into superior and inferior parts. The inferior mediastinum is further split into three: the anterior mediastinum in front of the pericardium, the middle mediastinum containing the pericardium, heart and great vessels, and the posterior mediastinum behind the pericardium.Slide 4

Superior Mediastinum

Boundaries: manubrium sterni (anterior); bodies of the upper four thoracic vertebrae (posterior); the plane of the thoracic inlet (superior); the plane through the sternal angle (inferior); mediastinal pleura (lateral).Slide 5

Arch of aorta; brachiocephalic artery; left common carotid artery; left subclavian artery.Slide 7

Anterior Mediastinum

Boundaries: body of sternum (anterior); pericardium (posterior); the transverse plane at the sternal angle (superior); diaphragm (inferior); mediastinal pleura (lateral). Contents: loose areolar tissue; superior and inferior sternopericardial ligaments; three or four lymph nodes; mediastinal branches of the internal thoracic artery; and, in children, the lower part of the thymus.Slides 13–14

Middle Mediastinum

Boundaries: flanked by the anterior and posterior mediastinum, the superior mediastinum above, and the diaphragm below. Contents: the heart and pericardium; ascending aorta; pulmonary trunk dividing into two pulmonary arteries; pericardiophrenic arteries; the lower half of the SVC; the terminal azygos vein; the right and left pulmonary veins; phrenic nerves and the deep cardiac plexus; tracheobronchial lymph nodes; and the tracheal bifurcation with the two principal bronchi.Slides 15–16

Posterior Mediastinum

Boundaries: pericardium, tracheal bifurcation and pulmonary vessels anteriorly; bodies of the lower eight thoracic vertebrae posteriorly; the thoracic-inlet plane superiorly; mediastinal pleura laterally; diaphragm inferiorly. Contents: oesophagus; thoracic duct; descending thoracic aorta and its branches; azygos, hemiazygos and accessory hemiazygos veins; vagus nerves; sympathetic trunks and splanchnic nerves; posterior mediastinal lymph nodes.Slides 17–18

Clinical Correlations

Common carotid artery: palpable on either side of the neck for pulse assessment; narrowing (carotid stenosis) can cause ischaemia or stroke. The carotid sinus and body sit at the bifurcation, housing baroreceptors (blood-pressure sensing) and chemoreceptors (blood-oxygen sensing).

Recurrent laryngeal nerve injury: because this nerve supplies nearly all intrinsic laryngeal muscles, surgical or diagnostic procedures in the superior mediastinum (e.g. mediastinotomy) — or disease there — can injure it and alter the voice.

Mediastinitis: inflammation of the mediastinum's loose connective tissue. Because the deep fascial planes of the neck continue down into the thorax, deep neck infections can track downward and produce mediastinitis.

The Lungs

Source deck: “The Lungs” — Slides 1–29

The lungs (pulmones) are the principal respiratory organs, sitting one on either side of the mediastinum within their own pleural sacs. Cone-shaped, their job is to exchange oxygen and carbon dioxide between inspired air and blood. The right lung is larger and heavier (~700 g) with three lobes; the left is slightly lighter (~650 g) with two lobes, since it makes room for the heart. Colour varies from rosy-pink in newborns and people living in clean air, to brown or black in smokers from accumulated carbon particles.Slide 3

External Features

Each lung has an apex, a base, three borders (anterior, posterior, inferior), and three surfaces (costal, medial, diaphragmatic).Slide 4

  • Apex: rounded; extends into the root of the neck about 3 cm above the first rib and 2.5 cm above the clavicle, capped by the cervical pleura and suprapleural membrane.
  • Base: semilunar and concave, resting on the diaphragm (hence "diaphragmatic surface"). On the right it is separated from the liver by the right diaphragmatic dome; on the left, from the spleen and stomach fundus by the left dome. The right base sits deeper because the liver pushes the right dome higher.Slide 5

Borders

The anterior border is thin and short; on the left lung it is interrupted by the cardiac notch (occupied by the heart and pericardium), below which projects a tongue-shaped lingula. The posterior border is thick and rounded, running from the C7 spinous process to the T10 spinous process. The inferior border is semilunar, separating the costal and medial surfaces.Slide 6

Surfaces

The costal surface is large, smooth and convex, covered by costal pleura and endothoracic fascia, and relates to the lateral chest wall — the upper six ribs at the midclavicular line, upper eight at the midaxillary line, and upper ten at the scapular line. The medial surface has a small posterior vertebral part and a larger anterior mediastinal part. The diaphragmatic surface is concave, deeper on the right (because of the underlying liver) and shallower on the left (because of the stomach and spleen).Slides 7–9

Lobes and Fissures

The right lung has three lobes separated by two fissures (oblique and horizontal); the left lung has two lobes separated by one oblique fissure.Slide 10

The right lung's oblique fissure runs obliquely down and forward, crossing the posterior border, while its horizontal fissure — unique to the right lung — starts from the oblique fissure at the midaxillary line and runs forward to the anterior border, separating the superior from the middle lobe.Slide 11

The left lung's oblique fissure separates its superior and inferior lobes and runs more vertically than its right-sided counterpart.Slide 12

Root and Hilum of the Lung

The root connects the lung's medial surface to the mediastinum and comprises the structures entering or leaving at the hilum, which lies opposite the T5–T7 vertebral bodies.Slide 13

Root of the left lung

Principal bronchus; two bronchial arteries and their veins; pulmonary artery; two pulmonary veins; lymphatics; anterior and posterior pulmonary nerve plexuses.

Root of the right lung

Eparterial (above the artery) and hyparterial (below the artery) bronchi; one bronchial artery; pulmonary artery; two pulmonary veins; lymphatics; anterior and posterior pulmonary nerve plexuses.

Internal Architecture — The Bronchial Tree

Internally each lung is built around the intrapulmonary bronchial tree: principal bronchus → lobar bronchi → (segmental bronchi) → terminal bronchioles → respiratory bronchioles.Slide 16

Trachea→ Principal bronchus→ Lobar bronchus→ Segmental bronchus→ Terminal bronchiole→ Respiratory bronchiole→ Alveoli

The trachea splits into right and left principal bronchi at the lower border of T4. The right principal bronchus is shorter, wider and more vertical (about 1 inch long, roughly 25° off the tracheal axis), while the left is narrower, longer and more horizontal (about 2 inches long, roughly 45° off axis).Slide 17

The right principal bronchus divides into three lobar bronchi (one per lobe); the left divides into two. Lobar bronchi give rise to segmental (tertiary) bronchi, which branch to terminal bronchioles, which in turn give off respiratory bronchioles — the first airway generation to lack cartilage and to feature thin-walled outpocketings, the alveoli, where gas exchange happens.Slide 18

Bronchopulmonary Segments

These are the structural and functional building blocks of the lung, each independently served by its own segmental bronchus and artery. Because each segment is a self-contained unit, disease such as tuberculosis can stay confined to one segment while sparing its neighbours — a fact exploited surgically in segmentectomy. The right lung has ten bronchopulmonary segments; the left typically has eight to ten. Each segment is supplied by a segmental bronchus and a segmental branch of the pulmonary artery, and drained by intersegmental veins.Slides 19–20

Right lung segments

Upper lobe: apical, posterior, anterior.
Middle lobe: lateral, medial.
Lower lobe: superior, medial basal, anterior basal, lateral basal, posterior basal.

Left lung segments

Upper lobe: apicoposterior, superior lingular, inferior lingular.
Lower lobe: superior, medial basal (variable), anterior basal, lateral basal, posterior basal.

Vasculature, Innervation & Lymphatics

The lungs receive a dual blood supply: pulmonary vessels (arteries and two veins per lung) for gas exchange, and bronchial arteries/veins for nutrition of the lung tissue itself. Innervation is parasympathetic (vagus nerve) and sympathetic (sympathetic trunks). Lymphatics arise from a superficial (subpleural) plexus draining the lung parenchyma and a deep plexus draining the root structures; both empty into the tracheobronchial nodes.Slide 23

The Path of Blood Oxygenation

Right ventricle→ Pulmonary trunk→ R & L pulmonary arteries→ Pulmonary capillaries→ Gas exchange at alveoli→ 4 pulmonary veins→ Left atrium

Deoxygenated blood leaves the right ventricle via the pulmonary trunk, which splits to carry blood to each lung; within the lung, arterial branches ramify down to capillary beds surrounding the alveoli, where oxygen diffuses into the blood and carbon dioxide diffuses out into the alveolar air for exhalation. Oxygen-rich blood then returns via the four pulmonary veins to the left atrium, completing the circuit.Slides 24–26

Clinical Correlations

Pulmonary hypertension: raised pulmonary venous pressure can compromise heart function; high left-atrial pressure can back up into the pulmonary veins and cause pulmonary congestion.

Pneumonia: infection fills the alveoli with fluid or pus, impairing gas exchange; it may stay localised or spread to involve whole lobes (lobar pneumonia).

COPD: airway narrowing from inflammation and mucus in the bronchi. Emphysema — enlarged air spaces with reduced surface area and lost elasticity — affects the alveoli and their supporting connective tissue.

Tuberculosis: forms granulomas, favouring the oxygen-rich upper lobes, and can scar tissue, cavitate, and stiffen the lung. Lung cancer: bronchogenic carcinomas begin in the bronchi, adenocarcinomas in the bronchioles/alveoli; tumours can obstruct airways and invade surrounding structures.

The Heart

Source deck: “Heart” — Slides 1–30

The heart is a hollow, muscular, roughly pyramidal organ housed in the mediastinum and wrapped by the pericardium, lying obliquely behind the sternum and adjoining costal cartilages. It has four chambers — right atrium, right ventricle, left atrium, left ventricle. Externally, the atrioventricular (coronary) groove separates atria from ventricles, and interventricular grooves separate the two ventricles. Average measurements: length 12 cm, width 9 cm, weight ~300 g in males and ~250 g in females.Slide 2

Relations of the Heart

  • Superior: aorta, SVC, pulmonary artery and veins
  • Inferior: diaphragm
  • Anterior: ribs, sternum, intercostal muscles
  • Posterior: oesophagus, trachea, right/left bronchi, descending aorta, IVC, thoracic vertebrae
  • Lateral: lungs

Slide 3

External Features

Apex, base, three surfaces (sternocostal, diaphragmatic, left) and four borders (right, left, upper, inferior).Slide 4

The apex, formed by the left ventricle, points down, forward and left, and lies at the level of the 5th left intercostal space, roughly 9 cm from the midline, just medial to the midclavicular line.Slide 5

The Pericardium

A double-walled sac enclosing the heart, made of a tough fibrous pericardium (dense, inelastic connective tissue that prevents overstretching, protects the heart, and anchors it in the mediastinum) and a thinner serous pericardium with two layers — an outer parietal layer fused to the fibrous pericardium, and an inner visceral layer that hugs the heart's own surface.Slides 11–12

Pericardial sinuses are lines of reflection between the visceral and parietal layers: the transverse pericardial sinus lies in front of the SVC and behind the ascending aorta/pulmonary trunk; the oblique pericardial sinus lies behind the heart within the pericardial sac.Slide 13

The Four Chambers

Atria receive blood into the heart; ventricles pump it out.Slide 14

Right Atrium

Forms the right heart border; receives blood from the SVC, IVC and coronary sinus. Wall thickness 2–3 mm; posterior wall smooth, anterior wall roughened by pectinate muscles. The interatrial septum separates the two atria and bears an oval depression, the fossa ovalis. Blood passes to the right ventricle through the tricuspid (three-cusped) valve, whose cusps anchor via chordae tendineae to papillary muscles.

Right Ventricle

Pumps blood to the lungs via the pulmonary trunk. Its inflow tract is roughened by trabeculae carneae; its outflow tract, the conus arteriosus (infundibulum), is smooth and leads to the pulmonary valve. Anterior, posterior and septal papillary muscles anchor the tricuspid cusps via chordae tendineae, preventing valve prolapse during systole. The moderator band (septomarginal trabecula) carries part of the right bundle branch to the anterior papillary muscle.

Left Atrium

Similar wall thickness to the right atrium; forms the base of the heart. Receives blood from the lungs via four pulmonary veins, and passes it to the left ventricle through the bicuspid (mitral) valve.

Left Ventricle

Pumps oxygenated blood into the aorta for systemic circulation; its walls are thicker than the right ventricle's to generate higher pressure. Inflow tract has trabeculae carneae; outflow tract is the aortic vestibule leading to the aortic valve. Two papillary muscles anchor the mitral valve via chordae tendineae.

Slides 15–17

Circulation of Blood Through the Heart

Functionally the heart is two pumps side by side. The right pump (right atrium + right ventricle) drives the pulmonary circulation; the left pump (left atrium + left ventricle) drives the systemic circulation.

SVC / IVC→ Right atrium→ Tricuspid valve→ Right ventricle→ Pulmonary trunk → lungs→ Pulmonary veins→ Left atrium→ Mitral valve→ Left ventricle→ Aorta → body

The atrioventricular valves stop blood regurgitating back into the atria during ventricular contraction.Slides 18–19

Valves of the Heart

Two pairs of valves prevent backflow: a pair of atrioventricular valves and a pair of semilunar valves.

  • Right AV valve (tricuspid): three cusps — anterior, posterior, septal — lying against the ventricular walls.
  • Left AV valve (bicuspid/mitral): two cusps — a larger anterior (aortic) cusp and a smaller posterior cusp.
  • Semilunar valves guard the pulmonary and aortic orifices, each with three cusps attached directly to the wall of the great vessel.

Arterial supply: right and left coronary arteries. Clinical Correlations
  • SA node dysfunction: failure of normal pacemaker activity can produce bradycardia.
  • AV node block (heart block): delayed or blocked conduction from atria to ventricles, often requiring an artificial pacemaker.
  • Coronary artery disease: blocked coronary arteries reduce myocardial blood flow and can precipitate myocardial infarction.
  • Pericarditis: pericardial inflammation causing chest pain typically eased by sitting forward; may progress to pericardial effusion.
  • Atrial/ventricular septal defects (ASD/VSD): abnormal openings allowing left-to-right shunting, risking heart failure or pulmonary hypertension if untreated.
  • Cardiomegaly: enlargement, especially of the left ventricle, seen in hypertension, valvular disease or heart failure, and detected on chest X-ray or echocardiogram.

The Bony Pelvis

Source deck: “Anatomy of the Pelvis” — Slides 1–42

The pelvis is the region encircled by the bony pelvic girdle, sitting inferoposterior to the abdomen and forming the transition zone between trunk and lower limb. The pelvic cavity is the lowest part of the abdominopelvic cavity, and the pelvis as a whole is subdivided into a greater and a lesser pelvis. Externally it is bordered by the inferior anterolateral abdominal wall in front, the gluteal region behind and to the sides, and the perineum below.Slides 3–4

Bones of the Pelvic Girdle

Three bones form the pelvic girdle in the mature skeleton: the right and left hip bones, plus the sacrum and coccyx. In children each hip bone is three separate bones (ilium, ischium, pubis) joined at the acetabulum by a Y-shaped (triradiate) cartilage; after puberty these fuse into one hip bone. The two hip bones meet in front at the pubic symphysis and behind the sacrum at the sacro-iliac joints.Slides 5–6

Ilium

The superior, widest, fan-shaped part of the hip bone — its "ala" (wing) is the fan's spread, its body the fan's handle, and the body helps form the acetabulum. The iliac crest runs the rim of the fan between the anterior and posterior superior iliac spines. The concave anteromedial surface of the ala is the iliac fossa; posteriorly the sacropelvic surface bears an auricular surface (synovial articulation with the sacrum) and an iliac tuberosity (ligamentous attachment).Slide 7

Ischium

Has a body (helping form the acetabulum) and a ramus (helping form the obturator foramen border). Key landmarks: the ischial tuberosity (large postero-inferior projection), the ischial spine (small pointed projection where ramus meets body), the lesser sciatic notch (between spine and tuberosity), and the greater sciatic notch (larger, above the spine, formed partly by the ilium).Slide 9

Pubis

An angulated bone with a superior ramus (contributing to the acetabulum) and an inferior ramus (bordering the obturator foramen). The pubic crest thickens the anterior body and ends laterally at the pubic tubercle; the superior ramus carries an oblique ridge, the pecten pubis (pectineal line).Slide 10

Sacrum

Five fused, wedge-shaped sacral vertebrae, wedged between the hip bones to form the roof and posterosuperior wall of the pelvic cavity. It transmits body weight to the pelvic girdle and has a base, apex, four surfaces (pelvic, dorsal, two lateral) and a sacral canal.Slide 11

Coccyx

A small triangular "tailbone" usually formed by fusion of four rudimentary coccygeal vertebrae (Co1 sometimes stays separate). Its short transverse processes connect to the sacrum; with age Co1 often fuses to the sacrum and the remaining segments fuse into one bone.Slide 12

Greater vs. Lesser Pelvis

An oblique plane through the pelvic inlet (superior pelvic aperture) splits the pelvis into the greater (false) pelvis, ringed by the upper pelvic girdle and occupied by lower abdominal viscera, and the lesser (true) pelvis, ringed by the lower pelvic girdle and providing the skeletal framework for both the pelvic cavity and the perineum.Slide 13

Pelvic Inlet and Outlet

The pelvic inlet's rim (the pelvic brim) is formed by the sacral promontory and ala, plus a right and left linea terminalis — the continuous ridge made of the arcuate line of the ilium, the pecten pubis and the pubic crest.Slide 14

The pelvic outlet is bounded by the pubic arch anteriorly, the ischial tuberosities laterally, the sacrotuberous ligaments posterolaterally, and the coccyx tip posteriorly. The pubic arch is formed where the right and left ischiopubic rami meet at the pubic symphysis, their lower borders defining the subpubic angle.Slide 15

The greater pelvis lies above the inlet, bounded by the iliac alae and the front of S1, and houses abdominal viscera such as loops of ileum and the sigmoid colon. The lesser pelvis lies between inlet and outlet, bounded by the pelvic surfaces of the hip bones, sacrum and coccyx, and contains the true pelvic cavity plus the deep perineal compartment (including the ischio-anal fossae).Slides 16–17

Sexual Dimorphism of the Pelvis

The pelvis shows the clearest skeletal sex differences in the body, reflecting men's typically heavier build and the female pelvis's adaptation (especially the lesser pelvis) for childbirth.Slide 18

Pelvic Joints & Ligaments

  • Sacro-iliac joints: strong, weight-bearing compound joints with only limited mobility.   • Pubic symphysis: a fibrocartilaginous interpubic disc (wider in women) plus surrounding ligaments joins the pubic bodies in the midline; superior and inferior pubic ligaments thicken the margins.Slide 22
  • Lumbosacral joints: the anterior L5/S1 intervertebral disc joint plus two posterior zygapophysial joints, reinforced by fan-shaped iliolumbar ligaments.Slide 23

Pelvic Walls and Floor

The pelvic cavity has an antero-inferior wall, two lateral walls, a posterior wall, and a floor.Slide 25

  • Antero-inferior wall: formed mainly by the pubic bodies/rami and the pubic symphysis; helps bear the weight of the bladder.
  • Lateral walls: each hip bone, including an obturator foramen closed by the obturator membrane; the obturator internus muscle pads most of this wall.Slide 26
  • Posterior wall: a bony midline component (sacrum and coccyx) plus musculoligamentous posterolateral walls formed by sacro-iliac ligaments and the piriformis muscles, which run from the sacrum to the greater trochanter and fill much of the greater sciatic foramen.Slide 27

Pelvic Floor — the Pelvic Diaphragm

A bowl- or funnel-shaped muscular sheet built from the coccygeus and levator ani muscles plus their covering fasciae. It lies within the lesser pelvis, separating the pelvic cavity above from the perineum below, forming the perineum's roof.Slide 28

Coccygeus arises from the lateral inferior sacrum/coccyx and attaches to the deep surface of the sacrospinous ligament. Levator ani attaches to the pubic bones anteriorly, the ischial spines posteriorly, and the tendinous arch between them; its urogenital hiatus — a gap between the muscle's medial borders — allows passage of the urethra and, in females, the vagina.Slide 29

Levator ani has three parts: puborectalis (thick, medial, forms a U-shaped sling behind the anorectal junction, bounding the urogenital hiatus); pubococcygeus (wider, thinner, lateral fibres to the coccyx and medial fibres forming part of the anococcygeal body, with slips extending to midline structures such as puboprostaticus, pubovaginalis and puboanalis); and iliococcygeus (thin, posterolateral, blending into the anococcygeal body).Slide 30

Together the levator ani muscles support the abdominopelvic viscera, resist rises in intra-abdominal pressure, and assist forced expiration, coughing, sneezing, vomiting, urination, defecation and trunk fixation during heavy lifting.Slide 32

Pelvic Arteries

Six main arteries enter the female lesser pelvis: the paired internal iliac and ovarian arteries, plus the unpaired median sacral and superior rectal arteries. In males, the testicular arteries stay outside the pelvis, so only four main arteries enter.Slide 33

The internal iliac artery, about 4 cm long, arises where the common iliac artery bifurcates (at the L5/S1 disc) and is the chief pelvic artery, supplying most pelvic viscera and part of the musculoskeletal pelvis. It usually ends at the greater sciatic foramen by splitting into anterior and posterior divisions.Slide 34

Tributaries of the internal iliac vein largely mirror the arteries (though no vein accompanies the umbilical artery). Pelvic innervation comes chiefly from the sacral and coccygeal spinal nerves along with the pelvic part of the autonomic nervous system.Slide 41

Clinical Correlations

Injury to pelvic nerves: during childbirth, the fetal head can compress the mother's sacral plexus, causing lower-limb pain; stretching of the nerve supply to levator ani can weaken pelvic-organ support, contributing to urinary or faecal incontinence.

Pelvic fractures: anteroposterior compression (e.g. a crush injury) commonly fractures the pubic rami.

Pelvic floor injury: during childbirth the pelvic floor supports the fetal head as the cervix dilates, risking tears of the perineum, levator ani (commonly pubococcygeus and puborectalis) and pelvic fascia, which can compromise support of the urethra, vagina and anal canal.

Small Intestine — Overview

The gastrointestinal segment following the stomach, roughly 6.5 m long in an average person, running from the pylorus to the ileocaecal junction, and the primary site of digestion and absorption. It comprises a fixed proximal part, the duodenum (about 25 cm/10 inches), followed by the freely mobile jejunum and ileum.Slide 3

Duodenum

The first part of the small intestine, beginning at the pyloric sphincter and curving posteriorly behind the peritoneum (i.e. retroperitoneal) in a C-shape around the pancreatic head before ascending back into the peritoneal cavity to meet the jejunum at the duodenojejunal junction. It receives chyme from the stomach and mixes it with bile and pancreatic enzymes.Slide 4

Jejunum & Ileum

These run on without a sharp demarcation from the duodenojejunal junction (left of L2) to the ileocolic junction near the right sacro-iliac joint, together measuring 6–7 m. Most jejunum sits in the left upper quadrant, most ileum in the right lower quadrant. Both are suspended from the posterior abdominal wall by the mesentery of the small intestine, giving them considerable mobility; the terminal ileum typically lies in the pelvis before ascending to meet the medial cecum.Slide 7

Sympathetic fibres arise from T10–T11 via splanchnic nerves and the superior mesenteric plexus; parasympathetic fibres come from the vagus via the coeliac and superior mesenteric plexuses.Slide 8

Large Intestine — Overview

About 1.5 m long, running from the cecum in the right iliac fossa to the anus. Apart from the transverse and sigmoid colon, it is more fixed in position than the small intestine. Its jobs: absorbing water to solidify faeces; storing, lubricating and expelling faeces; synthesising vitamin B-complex via resident bacterial flora; and protecting against microbial invasion through IgA-rich mucus.Slide 9

Its parts, in sequence: cecum, appendix, ascending colon, transverse colon, descending colon, sigmoid colon, rectum, anal canal.Slide 10

Cecum

The first part of the large intestine, a blind pouch roughly 6–7.5 cm in length and breadth, conical in shape and located in the right iliac fossa above the lateral half of the inguinal ligament; when distended with gas or faeces it may be palpable through the abdominal wall. It communicates upward with the ascending colon, medially at the ileocaecal junction with the ileum, and posteromedially with the appendix.Slide 11

Relations: anteriorly, coils of small intestine, greater omentum, and the anterior abdominal wall; posteriorly, right psoas major and iliacus, the femoral nerve, lateral cutaneous nerve of the thigh, genitofemoral nerve, and right gonadal vessels. The ileocaecal valve, between cecum and ileum, prevents reflux of colonic contents into the ileum.Slide 12

Vasculature: anterior/posterior caecal branches of the ileocolic artery (from the SMA); venous drainage to the superior mesenteric → portal system; lymph to ileocolic nodes → superior mesenteric pre-aortic nodes; sympathetic supply from T11–L1 via the superior mesenteric plexus, parasympathetic from both vagi.Slide 12

Clinical Note — Cecum

Failure of the ileocaecal sphincter to relax during prolonged large-bowel obstruction can distend and rupture the cecum if not relieved. Caecal volvulus — twisting of a mobile cecum on its mesentery — causes abdominal pain, distension and vomiting, and typically needs imaging plus surgical correction.

Slide 13

Appendix

A blind diverticulum, 6–10 cm long, packed with lymphoid tissue, arising from the posteromedial cecum just below the ileocaecal junction. It has its own short triangular mesentery (the meso-appendix), derived from the mesentery of the terminal ileum. Its position varies but is most often retrocecal.Slide 14

Vasculature: the appendicular artery (from the ileocolic artery); venous drainage via the ileocolic vein; lymph to ileocolic nodes and meso-appendix nodes; sympathetic supply from T10 via the lesser splanchnic nerve and superior mesenteric plexus, parasympathetic from the vagus.Slide 15

Clinical Note — Appendix

Appendicitis, inflammation of the appendix (often from luminal obstruction by faecoliths or oedema), is a common surgical emergency treated by appendectomy.

The Colon

Four parts — ascending, transverse, descending, sigmoid — arranged in an arch that encircles the small intestine: ascending colon to the right, transverse superior/anterior, descending to the left, sigmoid inferior.Slide 17

Ascending Colon

The second part of the large bowel, running up the right side of the abdomen from cecum to the right lobe of the liver, where it bends left at the right colic (hepatic) flexure — a bend lying deep to ribs 9–10 and overlapped by the liver. Narrower than the cecum, it is secondarily retroperitoneal, usually covered by peritoneum on its front and sides (about 25% of people have a short mesentery here). It is separated from the abdominal wall by the greater omentum, with the right paracolic gutter running along its lateral side.Slides 18–19

Vasculature: ileocolic and right colic arteries (from the SMA), which anastomose with the right branch of the middle colic artery to form the marginal (juxtacolic) artery running the length of the colon near its mesenteric border. Venous drainage via ileocolic and right colic veins to the SMV; lymph passes through epicolic and paracolic nodes, then ileocolic and intermediate right colic nodes, to the superior mesenteric nodes.Slides 20–21

Transverse Colon

The longest and most mobile part, crossing from the right colic flexure to the left colic flexure (which is typically higher, sharper and less mobile than the right), where it turns down into the descending colon. It lies in front of the lower left kidney and attaches to the diaphragm via the phrenicocolic ligament. Its mesentery, the transverse mesocolon, often loops down below the iliac crests and fuses to the back wall of the omental bursa; the mesocolon's root runs along the inferior pancreatic border. Position is variable — usually hanging to the level of the umbilicus (L3) but sometimes reaching into the pelvis in tall, thin people.Slides 22–23

Vasculature: middle colic artery, plus contributions from the right and left colic arteries, draining via the SMV; lymph to middle colic nodes → superior mesenteric nodes.Slides 24–25

Descending Colon

Retroperitoneal, running from the left colic flexure to the left iliac fossa where it becomes the sigmoid colon. Peritoneum covers it anteriorly and laterally, binding it to the posterior wall; about a third of people have a short mesentery here (usually too short to cause volvulus). It passes in front of the lateral border of the left kidney and has its own (left) paracolic gutter laterally.Slides 26–27

Sigmoid Colon

An S-shaped loop of variable length linking the descending colon and rectum, extending from the iliac fossa to S3, where it becomes the rectum (marked by the termination of the teniae coli, roughly 15 cm from the anus — the rectosigmoid junction). It runs first medially/superiorly along the external iliac vessels, then medially/inferiorly from the common iliac bifurcation to the front of the sacrum. The left ureter and the left common iliac artery's division lie retroperitoneally behind the root of the sigmoid mesocolon, which has an inverted-V attachment and is usually long.Slides 28–30

Vasculature (descending & sigmoid colon): left colic and sigmoid arteries from the inferior mesenteric artery; venous drainage via the inferior mesenteric vein (usually into the splenic vein, then the portal vein); parasympathetic supply via pelvic splanchnic nerves, sympathetic via lumbar splanchnic nerves; lymph to intermediate colic and inferior mesenteric nodes.Slide 31

Clinical Correlations

Colonic diverticulum: a pseudodiverticulum — a sac of mucosa and submucosa herniating through the muscular wall without carrying all bowel-wall layers.

Sigmoid volvulus: abnormal twisting of bowel that can obstruct it and cause severe complications; the sigmoid colon is the most common site for a volvulus.

The Rectum

Source deck: “The Rectum” — Slides 1–21

The rectum is the distal part of the large intestine between the sigmoid colon and the anal canal. Unlike the rest of the colon, it lacks teniae coli. It sits posteriorly in the lesser pelvis, in front of the lower three sacral vertebrae and the coccyx, and behind the bladder (males) or uterus (females). It measures roughly 12 cm long, with a diameter of about 4 cm when full (a transverse slit when empty).Slide 3

Course

Beginning just below the sigmoid colon, the rectum runs down and back; about 2.5 cm in front of and slightly below the coccyx's tip it turns to run vertically downward, then forward, becoming continuous with the anal canal at the anorectal junction — a level corresponding to the apex of the prostate.Slide 4

Peritoneal Relations

While the sigmoid colon is intraperitoneal, the rectum becomes retroperitoneal and then subperitoneal as it descends, giving it three peritoneal zones:

  • Superior third: peritoneum covers its anterior and lateral surfaces (retroperitoneal).
  • Middle third: peritoneum covers only the anterior surface.
  • Inferior third: entirely subperitoneal, with no peritoneal covering at all.Slides 5–6

In males, peritoneum reflects from the rectum onto the posterior bladder wall, forming the floor of the rectovesical pouch. In females it reflects onto the posterior vaginal fornix, forming the floor of the rectouterine pouch. In both sexes, lateral peritoneal reflections from the superior third form pararectal fossae, allowing the rectum room to distend as it fills.Slides 7–9

Curvatures

The rectum curves in both the anteroposterior and lateral planes.

  • Sacral curvature: follows the concavity of the sacrum and coccyx.
  • Perineal curvature: the forward bend at the anorectal junction, maintained by the puborectal sling of levator ani.Slide 10

Three lateral convexities exist — two to the right, one to the left: an upper lateral curve convex right at S3/S4, a middle lateral curve (the most prominent) convex left at the sacrococcygeal junction, and a lower lateral curve convex right at the coccyx tip.Slide 11

Interior of the Rectum

Two kinds of mucosal fold exist. Temporary folds are mostly longitudinal, found in the lower rectum, and disappear when the rectum distends with faeces. Permanent folds (rectal/Houston's valves) are semilunar transverse folds set against the lateral curvatures' concavities; they persist and become more prominent with distension, helping support faeces and prevent over-distension of the rectal ampulla.Slide 16

There are four permanent transverse folds: the first (upper) fold near the rectosigmoid junction; the second fold, about 2.5 cm below, projecting from the left wall at the upper lateral curve; the third valve, the largest, projecting from the anterior/right wall at the middle lateral curve near the top of the ampulla; and the fourth valve, about 2.5 cm below the third, projecting from the left wall at the lower lateral curve.Slide 17

Supports of the Rectum

The rectum is held in place and kept from prolapsing by the pelvic diaphragm (levator ani), the fascia of Waldeyer, the lateral rectal ligaments, the rectovesical (Denonvilliers') fascia, reflections of pelvic fascia from parietal to visceral layers, pelvirectal and ischiorectal fat, the pelvic peritoneum with its vascular pedicles, and the perineal body.Slide 18

Neurovasculature

Arteries: superior rectal, middle rectal, inferior rectal, and median sacral arteries. Veins: superior, middle and inferior rectal veins.Slide 19

Lymphatics: the upper half drains with the superior rectal vessels to inferior mesenteric and pararectal nodes; the lower half drains with the middle rectal vessels to internal iliac nodes. Innervation: sympathetic fibres from L1–L2, parasympathetic fibres from S2–S4, carried via splanchnic nerves.Slide 20

Clinical Correlations
  • Inflammatory bowel/Crohn's disease: inflammation of the rectal wall.
  • Rectocele: herniation of the rectum into the posterior vaginal wall, from a weakened rectovaginal septum (often post-childbirth); presents with vaginal bulge, constipation and difficulty defecating.
  • Haemorrhoids: dilatation of the rectal venous plexuses, causing bleeding and discomfort.
  • Rectal prolapse: protrusion of rectal mucosa or the full wall through the anus, from chronic straining or pelvic-floor weakness (especially in elderly patients or after childbirth); presents with a visible bulge and faecal incontinence.

Unit II · Topic 10

Kidney, Ureter & Suprarenal Gland

Source deck: “The Upper Urinary Organs” — Slides 1–41

The Kidneys

Bean-shaped, reddish-brown organs on the posterior abdominal wall, behind the peritoneum. On average, roughly 11 cm long, 6 cm wide and 3 cm thick, weighing about 150 g in males and 135 g in females.Slide 3

External features: a fibrous capsule; the renal pelvis (a funnel-shaped structure collecting urine and continuing as the ureter); and the renal hilum, a concave medial gap where the renal artery, vein and ureter connect.Slide 4

Positional differences: the left kidney sits closer to the midline than the right and is angled more obliquely, so the upper poles lie closer together than the lower poles. The upper poles sits roughly 2.5 cm from the midline, the hila about 5 cm, and the lower poles about 7.5 cm.Slide 5

External Morphology

Two poles (superior, inferior), two borders (lateral, medial), two surfaces (anterior, posterior). The superior pole is thick and rounded and sits nearer the midline than the pointed inferior pole; the anterior surface is convex and faces anterolaterally, while the posterior surface is flat and faces posteromedially.Slides 6–7

The medial border is convex near each pole and concave in the middle, sloping down and laterally with a vertical slit — the hilum — at its centre. The lateral border is simply convex.Slide 8

The hilum has anterior and posterior lips and transmits the renal vein, renal artery, renal pelvis, and a subsidiary branch of the renal artery.Slide 9

Relations of the Kidneys

Right kidney — anterior

Right suprarenal gland; right lobe of liver; second part of duodenum; hepatic (right colic) flexure; jejunum.

Left kidney — anterior

Left suprarenal gland; spleen; stomach; pancreas and splenic vessels; left colic flexure; jejunum.

Posteriorly, both kidneys relate to four muscles and three nerves, plus one rib (right) or two ribs (left).Slides 11–13

Capsules of the Kidney

From innermost to outermost: the fibrous capsule (a thin membrane closely investing the kidney); perirenal fat (adipose tissue around the fibrous capsule); renal fascia (a false capsule surrounding the kidney and perirenal fat); and pararenal fat (fat outside the renal fascia, cushioning the kidney).Slides 14–15

Internal Structure

The kidney proper has an outer cortex (just beneath the fibrous capsule) and an inner medulla made of 5–11 dark, cone-shaped renal pyramids, each tipped by a nipple-like renal papilla projecting into a minor calyx.Slide 17

The renal sinus is a substantial internal cavity opening at the hilum, its wall studded with renal papillae, and containing the minor and major calyces, renal pelvis, renal vessels, lymphatics, nerves and fat.Slide 18

The renal pelvis, a funnel-shaped continuation of the ureter, collects urine and passes it onward; it splits into three major calyces, which further divide into 5–11 minor calyces, each moulded around a renal papilla.Slide 19

Neurovasculature

Renal arteries supply the kidney via segmental → lobar → interlobar → arcuate arteries. Venous drainage is via the left and right renal veins, which leave the hilum anterior to the arteries and empty directly into the IVC. Lymph drains to para-caval and para-aortic nodes. Sympathetic fibres arise from T10–L1; parasympathetic fibres come from the vagus.Slide 20

Clinical Correlations — Kidney
  • Renal calculi (kidney stones): solid mineral/salt concretions forming in the kidney.
  • Horseshoe kidney: a congenital fusion of the lower poles into a U-shape across the midline.
  • Renal infarction: wedge-shaped pale areas of ischaemic necrosis, from embolism or thrombosis of renal artery branches.
  • Chronic pyelonephritis: small, shrunken, irregularly scarred kidneys with blunted calyces and thinned cortex, linked to recurrent UTIs, especially with vesicoureteral reflux in children.
  • Hydronephrosis: dilated renal pelvis and calyces from outflow obstruction (stones, tumours, congenital narrowing), with a swollen, thin-walled kidney.
  • Polycystic kidney disease: massively enlarged kidneys studded with fluid-filled cysts, risking hypertension, haematuria and renal failure.
Slide 21

The Ureter

A narrow, thick-walled, expansile muscular tube conveying urine from kidney to bladder by peristalsis, about 25 cm long and 3 mm in diameter, divided into the renal pelvis, an abdominal part, and a pelvic part.Slide 23

The renal pelvis is funnel-shaped, sitting at the renal hilum: its broader superior end continues from the major calyces, and its narrower inferior end gives rise to the ureter at the ureteropelvic junction.Slide 24

The abdominal part runs down and slightly medially over psoas major, which separates it from the transverse processes of the lumbar vertebrae.Slide 25

Clinical Correlations — Ureter

Ureteric colic: sudden, severe pain from ureteric obstruction, most often a kidney stone.

Vesicoureteral reflux (VUR): backward flow of urine from bladder into ureter (and sometimes kidney), typically a paediatric, often congenital, condition arising from a defective one-way valve mechanism at the oblique ureterovesical junction.

Suprarenal (Adrenal) Glands

Endocrine glands capping the upper pole of each kidney, each with an outer cortex and central medulla; dimensions approximately 50 mm × 30 mm × 10 mm, weighing about 5 g.Slides 32–33

Cushing syndrome: excess cortisol causing central obesity, moon face and hypertension, often from an adrenal tumour or prolonged steroid use. Adrenalectomy: surgical removal of one or both glands, used for adrenal tumours, Cushing syndrome or phaeochromocytoma.

Urinary Bladder & Urethra

Source deck: “Urinary Bladder & Urethra” — Slides 1–24

The urinary bladder is a hollow, muscular, distensible reservoir in the anterior pelvis. Empty, it sits behind the pubic symphysis within the true pelvis; filled, it can rise into the abdominal cavity above the pubic symphysis.Slide 2

Its shape varies with volume — tetrahedral when empty, ovoid when distended. Adult capacity ranges roughly 120–320 mL; urine beyond about 220 mL triggers the urge to void, though the bladder is usually emptied around 250–300 mL.Slide 3

External Features

Apex, base, neck, three surfaces (superior + two inferolateral) and four borders (anterior, posterior, two lateral).Slide 6

The apex anchors the median umbilical ligament (the fibrous remnant of the urachus) and sits behind the top of the pubic symphysis. The neck, the lowest, most fixed part, is where the inferolateral and posterior surfaces meet and is pierced by the urethra — resting on the prostate in males, related to the urogenital diaphragm in females.Slide 7

The base's upper part is separated from the rectum by the rectovesical pouch (containing small-bowel coils); its lower part is separated from the rectum by the terminal vas deferens and seminal vesicles (vas deferens lying medial to the vesicles), with the triangular zone between the two vasa separated from the rectum by the rectovesical (Denonvilliers') fascia. In females, the base is separated from the uterine cervix by the vesicouterine pouch.Slide 8

The superior surface is triangular, bounded by lateral borders running from the ureteric orifices back to the apex. The inferolateral surfaces slope down, forward and medially to meet anteriorly in the midline.Slide 9

Trigone of the Bladder

A smooth triangular zone on the posterior wall, bounded by the two ureteric orifices (posterolaterally) and the internal urethral orifice (inferiorly); its mucosa is smooth and firmly bound to the underlying muscle. In males, the uvula vesicae — a slight elevation behind the internal urethral orifice — reflects the underlying median lobe of the prostate.Slide 10

Trigone landmarks: the anteroinferior angle (internal urethral orifice); two posterosuperior angles (ureteric openings); the uvula vesicae; the interureteric ridge (bar of Mercier), connecting the two ureteric orifices along the trigone's superior boundary; and two uretero-urethral ridges (Bell's bars) running from each ureteric orifice toward the urethral orifice, produced by longitudinal ureteric fibres extending down toward the prostate's middle lobe.Slide 11

Support of the Bladder

Fixation is aided by the lateral, medial and lateral puboprostatic ligaments and the median umbilical ligament, plus posterior ligamentous support; the bladder neck is firmly anchored by its continuity with the prostate and urethra.Slide 12

Neurovasculature

Arteries: mainly branches of the internal iliac artery — superior vesical arteries (from the umbilical artery), the inferior vesical artery (males), and the vaginal artery (females). Venous drainage: a vesical venous plexus draining to the internal iliac veins. Lymphatics: external and internal iliac nodes. Nerves: sympathetic fibres (T11–L2) via the hypogastric plexus; parasympathetic fibres (S2–S4) via pelvic splanchnic nerves.Slide 13

Clinical Correlations — Bladder

Cystitis: bladder inflammation, usually infective, more common in females owing to their shorter urethra.

Trabeculated bladder: chronic outflow obstruction (e.g. enlarged prostate, urethral stricture) causes the bladder to distend and its muscle bundles to hypertrophy and interlace, producing an open-weave "trabeculated" wall appearance.

The Male Urethra

Conveys urine and, during ejaculation, semen; about 18–20 cm long, running from the internal urethral orifice at the bladder neck to the external urethral orifice at the glans tip. Flaccid, it is S-shaped (two curves); erect, the distal curve straightens out, giving a J-shape. It has four parts.Slide 16

Clinical Correlations — Male Urethra
  • Hypospadias: urethral opening on the underside (ventral) of the penis; epispadias: opening on the dorsal side.
  • Urethral stricture: narrowing (often in the bulbar urethra) from trauma, infection or inflammation, causing difficulty urinating, weak stream, and retention.
  • Urethritis: inflammation, usually from a sexually transmitted infection, with burning on urination and discharge.
Slide 21

The Female Urethra

Conducts urine to the exterior; about 4 cm long — much shorter than the male urethra — embedded in the anterior vaginal wall, running from the internal to the external urethral orifice (the latter lying between the clitoris and vaginal orifice). It passes down and forward from the bladder neck, pierces the urogenital diaphragm, and lies anterior to the vagina throughout its course.Slide 22

In cross-section its shape changes along its length: crescentic (convexity forward) proximally, star-shaped in the middle, a transverse slit distally, and a sagittal slit at the external orifice.Slide 23

Clinical Correlations — Female Urethra

UTIs are markedly more common in females because of the short urethra and its orifice's proximity to the vaginal and anal openings; regular hygiene is advised. Urinary incontinence is also more common, since the external urethral sphincter is a delicate structure that can be further weakened by childbirth.

Slide 23

Urethral Neurovasculature

Male: supplied by the inferior vesical, internal pudendal and dorsal penile arteries. Female: supplied by the internal pudendal, vaginal and inferior vesical arteries. Venous drainage: prostatic venous plexus (males) or vesical/vaginal venous plexuses (females). Lymphatics: internal iliac and inguinal nodes. Nerves: autonomic fibres via the inferior hypogastric plexus — sympathetic fibres control the internal sphincter and assist ejaculation, parasympathetic fibres relax urethral smooth muscle during voiding — plus somatic innervation of the external sphincter via the pudendal nerve (S2–S4).Slide 24

Male Genital Organs

Source deck: “Male Genital Organ” — Slides 1–13

Prostate

A pyramidal, fibromuscular glandular organ surrounding the prostatic urethra, secreting acid phosphatase, fibrinolysin, citric acid, amylase, prostate-specific antigen (PSA) and prostaglandins — secretions that form the bulk of seminal fluid. It sits in the lesser pelvis below the bladder neck and above the urogenital diaphragm, behind the lower pubic symphysis and in front of the rectal ampulla, flanked on each side by levator ani. Shaped like an inverted cone (or a chestnut), it weighs about 3 g, with a base width of 4 cm, length 3 cm and thickness 2 cm.Slide 2

Capsule and Internal Anatomy

A dense fibromuscular true capsule directly invests the gland; a false capsule (prostatic sheath), from the visceral pelvic fascia, lies outside it, with the prostatic venous plexus sandwiched between the two. The prostatic urethra runs vertically through the gland, its posterior wall bearing the urethral crest, the seminal colliculus (a central elevation with the ejaculatory duct openings), and prostatic sinuses where the prostatic ducts open.Slide 4

Roughly 20–30 prostatic ducts empty into the prostatic urethra, secreting a milky, mildly alkaline fluid rich in citric acid, zinc and enzymes such as PSA — contributing about 30% of semen volume.Slide 5

Surfaces: anterior (narrow, behind the pubic symphysis); posterior (flat, related to the rectum); inferolateral (resting on levator ani).Slide 5

Neurovasculature: arterial supply from branches of the inferior vesical, middle rectal and internal pudendal arteries; venous drainage via the prostatic venous plexus; lymphatics to internal iliac, external iliac and sacral nodes; sympathetic supply via the superior hypogastric plexus, parasympathetic via pelvic splanchnic nerves.Slide 6

Clinical Correlations — Prostate

Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH): after age 50, hypertrophy of the median lobe forms an adenoma that compresses the prostatic urethra and obstructs urine flow.

Prostatic carcinoma: usually occurs after age 50, most often in the outer (peripheral) zone, typically involving the posterior lobe.

Slide 7

Vas Deferens (Ductus Deferens)

A paired, thick-walled muscular tube carrying spermatozoa from the epididymis to the ejaculatory duct — about 45 cm long, narrow-lumened except for its terminal, sacculated segment, the ampulla.Slide 8

External course: begins at the testis's inferior pole as a continuation of the epididymal duct, ascends behind the testis and medial to the epididymis, enters the spermatic cord (lying in its posterior part — the usual site for vasectomy), passes through the inguinal canal, and enters the abdomen through the deep inguinal ring lateral to the inferior epigastric artery.Slide 9

Internal course: loops around the inferior epigastric artery into the lesser pelvis, descends along the lateral pelvic wall crossing the obliterated umbilical artery and the obturator nerve/vessels, crosses above and medial to the ureter, bends down and medially behind the bladder and medial to the seminal vesicle, enlarges into the ampulla near the bladder base, and finally joins the seminal vesicle duct to form the ejaculatory duct just behind the bladder neck.Slide 10

Arteries: branches to the vas deferens from the superior vesical, inferior vesical and middle rectal arteries. Venous drainage: vesical venous plexus. Nerves: pelvic splanchnic nerves. Lymphatics: external, internal and common iliac nodes.Slide 11

Clinical Note — Vasectomy

A common method of male sterilisation; ejaculate becomes sperm-free roughly a week afterward, preventing pregnancy, while ongoing sperm production is absorbed within the epididymis.

Slide 11

Seminal Vesicles

Paired coiled, sacculated tubes about 5 cm long (unravelling to roughly three times that length), lying extraperitoneally at the bladder base, lateral to the terminal vas deferens and in front of the rectum. Each vesicle's narrow lower end (its duct) joins the ductus deferens to form the ejaculatory duct. Their secretion is mildly alkaline, rich in fructose, choline and vesiculose.Slide 12

Vasculature: inferior vesical and middle rectal arteries and veins.Slide 12

Clinical Note — Seminal Vesicle Secretion

Fructose, unique to seminal vesicle secretion elsewhere in the body, offers forensic evidence of seminal fluid presence, though choline crystals (the Florence test) are the preferred forensic marker.

Slide 12

Ejaculatory Ducts

Paired ducts, one either side of the midline at the lower bladder base, roughly 1–2 cm long, formed by the union of the seminal vesicle duct and the ampulla of the vas deferens. They pierce the posterior half of the prostate and open into the prostatic urethra at the seminal colliculus (verumontanum).Slide 13

Vasculature: arteries usually branch from the superior vesical arteries; veins join the prostatic and vesical venous plexuses.Slide 13

The Female Pelvis

Source deck: “Female Pelvis” — Slides 1–28

This topic covers the female urethra and the female internal genital organs — ovaries, uterine tubes, uterus, vagina — plus the vulva.Slide 2

Female Urethra

Roughly 4 cm long and 6 mm in diameter, it runs anteroinferiorly from the internal urethral orifice, behind and then below the pubic symphysis, to the external urethral orifice. It lies in front of the vagina, raising a ridge in the anterior vaginal wall, and passes with the vagina through the pelvic diaphragm, external urethral sphincter, and perineal membrane. Urethral glands cluster especially in its upper part; one paired group, the paraurethral (Skene's) glands, are the female homolog of the prostate.Slide 2

Vasculature: internal pudendal and vaginal arteries, with correspondingly named veins. Innervation: from the vesical plexus and the pudendal nerve.Slide 3

Ovaries

Almond-shaped and -sized female gonads where oocytes develop; also endocrine organs producing reproductive hormones. Each ovary hangs from a short peritoneal fold, the mesovarium, with its vessels, lymphatics and nerves crossing the pelvic brim within a peritoneal fold to reach its superolateral aspect.Slide 4

Uterine Tubes

Roughly 10 cm long, carrying the monthly-released oocyte from the periovarian peritoneal cavity to the uterine cavity, and serving as the usual site of fertilisation. They lie in a narrow mesentery, the mesosalpinx, which forms the free upper edge of the broad ligament.Slide 5

Uterus

A thick-walled, pear-shaped, hollow muscular organ where the embryo/fetus develops; its muscular wall stretches to accommodate growth and later powers expulsion during childbirth. The non-pregnant uterus normally lies in the lesser pelvis, its body resting on the bladder and its cervix between the bladder and rectum.Slide 10

It divides into a body (upper two-thirds, including the fundus above the tubal openings, freely movable between the layers of the broad ligament, with vesical and intestinal surfaces) and a cervix (the narrower, cylindrical lower third, about 2.5 cm long, itself split into a supravaginal part and a vaginal part that projects into the vagina). The isthmus demarcates body from cervix.Slide 11

Uterine Wall Layers

  • Perimetrium — outer serosal coat (peritoneum plus a thin connective-tissue layer)
  • Myometrium — middle smooth-muscle coat, which stretches dramatically in pregnancy
  • Endometrium — inner mucosal coat, firmly bonded to the myometrium, changing structure through the menstrual cycle

Slide 12

Relations: anteriorly, the vesicouterine pouch and bladder's superior surface; posteriorly, the rectouterine pouch (containing small-bowel loops) and the front of the rectum; laterally, the peritoneal broad ligament and, lower down beside the cervix and vagina, the fascial cardinal ligaments.Slide 13

Vasculature: mainly the uterine arteries, with collateral supply from the ovarian arteries; venous drainage via uterine veins entering the broad ligaments to form a uterine venous plexus beside the cervix.Slide 13

Vagina

A distensible musculomembranous tube, 7–9 cm long, running from the mid-cervix to the vaginal orifice; its upper end surrounds the cervix. Functions: conducting menstrual flow; forming the lower birth canal; receiving the penis and ejaculate during intercourse; and communicating with the cervical canal above and the vestibule below.Slide 14

Usually collapsed with its lateral walls touching along an anteroposterior slit, the vagina lies behind the bladder and urethra (the urethra projecting into its lower anterior wall) and in front of the rectum, passing between the medial margins of the puborectalis part of levator ani. Four muscles compress it as sphincters: pubovaginalis, external urethral sphincter, urethrovaginal sphincter, and bulbospongiosus.Slide 15

Relations: anteriorly, the bladder fundus and urethra; laterally, levator ani, visceral pelvic fascia and the ureters; posteriorly (inferior to superior), the anal canal, rectum and rectouterine pouch.Slide 16

Vasculature: the upper vagina is supplied by the uterine arteries; the middle and lower vagina by the vaginal and internal pudendal arteries. Venous plexuses along the vaginal walls and mucosa merge with the uterine plexus as the uterovaginal plexus, draining to the internal iliac veins via the uterine vein.Slides 16–17

Innervation: only the lowest fifth to quarter of the vagina is somatically innervated, via the deep perineal nerve (a pudendal branch) carrying sympathetic and visceral afferent — but no parasympathetic — fibres. The remaining upper three-quarters to four-fifths is visceral in innervation, supplied (along with the uterus) by the uterovaginal nerve plexus.Slide 17

The Vulva

The collective term for the external female genitalia, serving three roles: sensory tissue during intercourse, directing urine flow during micturition, and protecting the internal reproductive tract from infection.Slide 18

Clinical Correlations

Genital tract infection: because the tract communicates with the peritoneal cavity via the abdominal ostia of the uterine tubes, vaginal, uterine or tubal infection can result in peritonitis.

Hysterosalpingography: a radiographic study of tubal patency, injecting contrast or CO₂ gas into the uterus and tubes via the external cervical os.

Tubal ligation: a surgical birth-control method; oocytes entering the ligated tubes degenerate and are absorbed.

Bicornuate uterus: incomplete fusion of the embryonic paramesonephric ducts can yield anomalies ranging from a unicornuate uterus (one duct only) to a duplicated bicornuate uterus.

Hysterectomy: surgical removal of the uterus, common given the frequency of uterine and cervical cancer, performed via the abdominal wall or through the vagina.

Vaginal distension: the vagina, especially the posterior fornix region, distends markedly during intercourse and even more so during childbirth, particularly in the anteroposterior direction as the fetal shoulders deliver.
