Analytical Cytology

The biochemical techniques used to pull cells apart and isolate their components for analysis — the lab side of Module 1.

STEP 1

Cell Fractionation

Isolating individual cellular components from a tissue sample so each can be studied on its own.

Homogenization

MODULE 3

Water and Fluid Compartments

The physical properties that make water essential to biochemistry, how it's distributed across the body, and what keeps that balance in check.

Water, the Solvent of Life

Water's unique physical and chemical properties — polarity, hydrogen bonding, and heat capacity — make it the medium every biochemical reaction depends on.

Body Fluid Compartments

Relative distribution of water across the body's major compartments.

Total Body Water (TBW)~60% of body weight
Intracellular Fluid (ICF)~40% of body weight
Fluid held inside cells — roughly two-thirds of TBW.
Extracellular Fluid (ECF)~20% of body weight
Fluid outside cells — plasma and interstitial fluid combined, roughly one-third of TBW.
Hormonal Regulation of Water Balance

ADH

Antidiuretic hormone signals the kidneys to reabsorb more water, concentrating urine and conserving body water when it's needed.

Aldosterone

Drives sodium reabsorption in the kidneys, and water follows sodium — helping regulate both fluid volume and blood pressure.

ANP

Atrial natriuretic peptide is released when the heart senses excess volume, promoting sodium and water loss to bring it back down.

Clinical Correlates

Dehydration

Signs and symptoms that appear when fluid losses outpace intake — a direct consequence of the balance above tipping one way.

Overhydration

Signs and symptoms that appear when fluid intake or retention outpaces the body's ability to clear it.

MODULE 4

Acid–Base Balance

The chemistry behind why pH matters, and the buffer systems that hold it steady.

The pH Scale

pH = −log[H♠] — a log scale measuring hydrogen ion concentration in a fluid.

Effects on Enzymes & Cellular Processes

Enzymes and cellular reactions are shaped for a narrow pH range; drift outside it and their activity falls off sharply.

Biological Buffers

Buffer systems in biological fluids resist pH change by absorbing excess acid or base, keeping conditions stable for enzymes to function.
