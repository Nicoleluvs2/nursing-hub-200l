Neuroanatomy Study Notes

Paraphrased study notes built from Clinically Oriented Anatomy, 8th ed. (Moore, Agur & Dalley) — organized to match your Anatomy II course outline. Page numbers refer to the PDF page count.

Important scope note Moore's textbook is a regional/gross anatomy text, not a dedicated neuroanatomy text — the book says so itself on p. 1658. So a few outline items simply aren't covered here in the depth your course wants:
  • Ascending/descending spinal tracts (spinothalamic, corticospinal, dorsal columns) — not covered at the pathway level anywhere in this book.
  • Glial cell subtypes (astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, microglia) — not named individually; the book only discusses neurons, nerve fibers, and neuroglia in general terms.
  • Basal ganglia detail and lobe functions — mentioned only in passing as gross structures, not their circuitry or functional roles.
For those three topics you'll need your lecture notes, Pathoma-style pathway diagrams, or a neuroanatomy-specific text (e.g., Snell's). Everything else below is solidly covered.

1. Organization of the Nervous System pp. 152–185

CNS vs. PNS p. 172

The nervous system splits functionally into two divisions. The central nervous system (CNS) is the brain and spinal cord. The peripheral nervous system (PNS) is everything else — the nerve fibers and their cell bodies that live outside the CNS, organized into cranial and spinal nerves that link the CNS to the rest of the body.

A cluster of nerve cell bodies is called a nucleus when it's inside the CNS, and a ganglion when it's outside the CNS in the periphery. Similarly, a bundle of connecting fibers is a tract inside the CNS, but a nerve once it's in the periphery (nerve = fiber bundle + connective tissue wrapping + its own blood supply, the vasa nervorum).

Gray Matter and White Matter p. 152

Both the brain and spinal cord are built from two tissue types: gray matter, made of neuron cell bodies, and white matter, made of the myelinated fiber tracts connecting those cell bodies. In a spinal cord cross-section, the gray matter forms a distinctive H-shape (or butterfly shape) sitting inside a surrounding matrix of white matter. The "arms" of the H are called horns — there are paired posterior (dorsal) horns and anterior (ventral) horns.

Nerve Fiber Structure pp. 154–157

An individual nerve fiber is an axon wrapped in a neurolemma (a sheath of Schwann cell membrane) plus a thin layer of endoneurial connective tissue. Fibers come in two flavors:

  • Myelinated fibers — a continuous chain of Schwann cells wraps each axon individually, laying down myelin.
  • Unmyelinated fibers — several axons share space inside one Schwann cell, and no myelin is produced. Most cutaneous (skin) sensory fibers fall into this category.

Zooming out, a whole nerve is built like a telephone cable: individual insulated axons are bundled into fascicles wrapped in perineurium, and the fascicles themselves are bundled together inside an outer wrapping called the epineurium. A separate, even finer layer — the endoneurium — surrounds each individual axon.

Cranial vs. Spinal Nerves pp. 157–159

Cranial nerves exit through skull foramina (11 of the 12 pairs actually arise from the brain; CN XI arises from the superior spinal cord). Spinal nerves exit through intervertebral foramina — there are 31 pairs, each initially forming as rootlets that converge into an anterior (motor) root and a posterior (sensory) root. These two roots join to form a single mixed spinal nerve, which then splits into anterior and posterior rami.

The Autonomic Nervous System (ANS) pp. 173–185

The ANS is the visceral motor system — it drives smooth muscle, modified cardiac muscle, and glands, and it always uses a two-neuron relay (unlike somatic motor pathways, which use just one neuron). A presynaptic neuron starts in the CNS and synapses onto a postsynaptic neuron sitting in a peripheral ganglion; the postsynaptic neuron's fiber then reaches the target organ.

Sympathetic distribution is remarkably wide — postsynaptic sympathetic fibers travel with virtually every branch of every spinal nerve, so this system reaches almost every part of the body (the exceptions are avascular tissues like cartilage and nails). Its main job is regulating blood vessel tone (vasoconstriction/vasodilation), plus controlling sweating and the arrector pili muscles that produce goosebumps.

Parasympathetic distribution is much more restricted — only to the head, the visceral cavities of the trunk, and the erectile tissue of the external genitalia. It never reaches the body wall or limbs. Notably, even though the parasympathetic system anatomically has a "cranial" and a "sacral" outflow, the cranial outflow via the vagus nerve (CN X) actually dominates — it alone supplies all thoracic viscera and most of the GI tract down to the left colic flexure. The sacral outflow (S2–4) only picks up the descending colon, sigmoid colon, and rectum.

The suprarenal (adrenal) medulla is an exception to the usual two-neuron sympathetic relay: its secretory cells act like a modified postsynaptic neuron with no axon, so they're innervated directly by presynaptic fibers and release their neurotransmitter straight into the bloodstream — producing a body-wide sympathetic surge rather than a localized effect. p. 180

Note: the four possible course options presynaptic sympathetic fibers can take once they reach the sympathetic trunk (ascend, descend, synapse immediately, or pass straight through to a prevertebral ganglion) is classic exam material — see p. 176 in the source.

2. The Meninges & Cerebrospinal Fluid pp. 1636–1673, 982–991

The Three Meningeal Layers p. 1636

From outside in: dura mater (tough, thick, fibrous), arachnoid mater (thin, web-like middle layer), and pia mater (delicate, vascularized, hugs the brain surface directly). The arachnoid and pia together are called the leptomeninges — they develop from a single embryonic layer, which is why fine strands (arachnoid trabeculae) still bridge between them in the adult. The CSF-filled subarachnoid space sits between arachnoid and pia; this is the one meningeal "space" that's actually real anatomically (more on that below).

Cranial Dura Mater — Two Layers pp. 1638–1639

Unlike spinal dura (a single layer), cranial dura is bilaminar: an outer periosteal layer fused to the inside of the skull, and an inner meningeal layer. They're tightly fused everywhere except where they separate to form dural infoldings or to enclose the venous sinuses.

Dural Infoldings (Reflections) pp. 1639–1643

  • Falx cerebri — the largest fold; sits in the longitudinal fissure between the two cerebral hemispheres.
  • Tentorium cerebelli — a tent-shaped shelf separating the occipital lobes above from the cerebellum below; divides the cranial cavity into supratentorial and infratentorial compartments. Its free anteromedial edge creates the tentorial notch, the gap the brainstem passes through.
  • Falx cerebelli — small vertical fold partially separating the two cerebellar hemispheres.
  • Diaphragma sellae — smallest fold; roofs over the pituitary fossa, with a small opening for the infundibulum.

Dural Venous Sinuses pp. 1643–1646

These are endothelium-lined channels running between the two dural layers, draining venous blood from the brain toward the internal jugular veins. Key ones to know: superior and inferior sagittal sinus → straight sinus → confluence of sinuses → paired transverse sinuses → sigmoid sinuses → internal jugular vein. The cavernous sinus deserves special attention — it flanks the pituitary fossa and has the internal carotid artery and CN VI running straight through it, with CN III, IV, and the first two divisions of CN V embedded in its lateral wall.

Cavernous sinus thrombosis can result from facial infections (the "danger triangle" from the nose to the upper lip drains toward the cavernous sinus via valveless veins), and can compress or damage any of the cranial nerves running through/alongside the sinus. p. 1655

Meningeal "Spaces" — a common exam trap p. 1651

Only one of the three commonly-named meningeal spaces is a real, naturally occurring space:

  • Epidural/extradural "space" — not real; the dura is normally stuck to the skull. It only becomes an actual space pathologically (e.g., an epidural hematoma from a torn meningeal vessel).
  • "Subdural space" — also not real; it's a plane of weak dural border cells that can split open with trauma, creating a pathological space (subdural hematoma).
  • Subarachnoid space — this one is genuinely present at all times, filled with CSF, trabecular cells, and blood vessels.

Ventricular System pp. 1660–1662

Four fluid-filled cavities inside the brain: paired lateral ventricles (1st and 2nd) inside the cerebral hemispheres, connecting via the interventricular foramina to the midline 3rd ventricle, which connects via the narrow cerebral aqueduct (running through the midbrain) to the 4th ventricle between the pons/medulla and cerebellum. CSF leaves the 4th ventricle into the subarachnoid space through one median and two lateral apertures — the only way CSF gets out of the ventricular system.

CSF: Production, Circulation, Absorption pp. 1663–1664

  • Secretion — about 400–500 mL/day, mainly by the choroid plexuses (modified ependymal cells) inside the lateral, 3rd, and 4th ventricles.
  • Circulation — lateral ventricles → 3rd ventricle → cerebral aqueduct → 4th ventricle → subarachnoid space (via the apertures) → flows around the brain and spinal cord through the subarachnoid cisterns and sulci.
  • Absorption — mainly through arachnoid granulations, tuft-like projections of arachnoid that poke into the dural venous sinuses (especially the superior sagittal sinus) and transfer CSF back into venous blood.

Spinal Meninges & CSF pp. 982–987

The same three-layer pattern continues around the spinal cord, with a few differences from the cranial version: spinal dura is a single layer (not bilaminar), and it's separated from the vertebral bone by a genuine, fat-filled epidural space containing the internal vertebral venous plexus — this is a real space in the spine, unlike the cranial "epidural space." The lumbar cistern (from L2 down to S2) is the enlarged subarachnoid space containing the cauda equina and CSF — this is the target for a lumbar puncture.

Hydrocephalus results from CSF overproduction, obstruction of its flow, or impaired absorption. The most common obstruction site is the cerebral aqueduct or an interventricular foramen ("obstructive hydrocephalus"). In "communicating hydrocephalus," flow through the ventricles is fine, but movement of CSF into the venous system is blocked (e.g., arachnoid granulations obstructed by red blood cells after a subarachnoid hemorrhage). pp. 1672–1673
Meningitis is inflammation of the leptomeninges (arachnoid + pia), usually from bacterial or viral infection reaching the subarachnoid space via the bloodstream, or via direct spread from a skull/sinus fracture. A CSF leak from the nose (rhinorrhea) or ear (otorrhea) after a basal skull fracture raises meningitis risk because it creates a direct route for pathogens to reach the meninges. pp. 1656, 1673

3. The Spinal Cord pp. 976–993

Gross Anatomy pp. 976–978

The spinal cord is a direct continuation of the medulla oblongata, beginning at the foramen magnum. In adults it's about 42–45 cm long, tapering inferiorly to a cone-shaped tip, the conus medullaris, which usually ends around the L1–L2 vertebral level (though it can range from T12 to L3). This means the spinal cord only occupies the superior two-thirds of the vertebral canal.

Two enlargements correspond to limb innervation:

  • Cervical enlargement (C4–T1) — gives rise to the brachial plexus for the upper limb.
  • Lumbosacral enlargement (T11–S1) — gives rise to the lumbar and sacral plexuses for the lower limb.

Why the Cord Doesn't Fill the Canal — Developmental Note pp. 979–981

In the embryo, the spinal cord fills the entire vertebral canal. But the vertebral column grows faster than the cord during fetal life, so the cord effectively "ascends" relative to the bony canal. By birth, the conus medullaris sits around L4–L5; in the adult it's crept even higher to ~L1–L2. Because of this mismatch, the lower spinal nerve roots have to travel a long way down inside the canal before exiting at their proper vertebral level — this bundle of long descending roots is the cauda equina ("horse's tail"). The filum terminale (a fibrous remnant of the embryonic cord) runs down through the cauda equina and anchors the cord's inferior end to the coccyx.

Numbering Spinal Nerves vs. Vertebrae p. 978

Cervical nerves C1–C7 exit above their same-numbered vertebra, but C8 exits below C7 (there's no C8 vertebra) — from that point down, every spinal nerve exits below its same-numbered vertebra.

Spinal Meninges pp. 983–986

Covered in Section 2 above in detail. Key spinal-specific structures: the denticulate ligaments (paired sawtooth-shaped pial extensions that anchor the cord laterally within the dural sac) and the dural root sheaths (tapering dural extensions that follow each nerve root out toward the intervertebral foramen, eventually blending with the nerve's epineurium).

Vasculature pp. 987–990

Three longitudinal arteries run the length of the cord: one anterior spinal artery (formed by branches of the vertebral arteries, supplies roughly the anterior two-thirds of the cord via small sulcal branches) and paired posterior spinal arteries. These alone can't supply the whole cord length, so they're reinforced at intervals by segmental medullary arteries that enter via the intervertebral foramina. The largest of these reinforcing vessels is the great anterior segmental medullary artery (of Adamkiewicz), usually on the left, which is the major blood supply to the lumbosacral enlargement.

Because the artery of Adamkiewicz supplies such a large territory, injury or occlusion (e.g., during aortic cross-clamping in surgery, or a ruptured aortic aneurysm) can cause spinal cord ischemia and paraplegia. p. 993
Lumbar puncture is performed between L3–L4 or L4–L5 (below the cord's termination, so there's no risk of hitting the cord itself), with the needle passing through skin, ligamentum flavum, dura, and arachnoid to reach the lumbar cistern. Spinal anesthesia injects into the subarachnoid space itself; epidural anesthesia injects into the epidural space (not through the dura). pp. 991–993

4. The Brainstem & Cerebellum pp. 1658–1660

Moore's book treats the brainstem and cerebellum only at the level of gross structure and their relationship to the cranial fossae and cranial nerves — not internal circuitry. Here's what it does cover:

Medulla Oblongata p. 1659

The most caudal part of the brainstem, continuous inferiorly with the spinal cord; sits in the posterior cranial fossa. CN IX, X, and XII attach here, and CN VI–VIII attach at the pons–medulla junction.

The Pons p. 1659

Sits between the midbrain above and the medulla below, in the anterior part of the posterior cranial fossa. CN V attaches here.

The Midbrain p. 1659

The rostral (most superior) part of the brainstem, positioned at the junction of the middle and posterior cranial fossae. CN III and IV attach here. The narrow cerebral aqueduct runs through it, connecting the 3rd and 4th ventricles.

The Cerebellum p. 1659

A large mass posterior to the pons and medulla, sitting beneath the tentorium cerebelli in the posterior cranial fossa. It has two lateral hemispheres joined by a narrow central strip, the vermis. Venous drainage is via superior and inferior cerebellar veins into the transverse and sigmoid sinuses.

What's not here: the internal nuclei of the brainstem, the specific tracts passing through it, or the cerebellar peduncles/circuitry — those are neuroanatomy-course territory, not gross-anatomy territory.

5. The Cerebrum pp. 1658–1659

Hemispheres and Lobes p. 1658

The cerebrum includes the cerebral hemispheres and basal ganglia. The two hemispheres are separated by the longitudinal cerebral fissure (with the falx cerebri sitting in it) and are each divided into four lobes:

  • Frontal lobe — anterior to the central sulcus; occupies the anterior cranial fossa.
  • Parietal lobe — posterior to the central sulcus.
  • Temporal lobe — inferior to the lateral sulcus; occupies the lateral part of the middle cranial fossa.
  • Occipital lobe — posterior-most; extends back over the tentorium cerebelli.

The boundary between parietal and occipital lobes is the parieto-occipital sulcus, visible on the brain's medial surface once it's been hemisected.

Diencephalon p. 1659

Forms the central core of the brain and is made up of the epithalamus, dorsal thalamus, and hypothalamus.

In the visual pathway, fibers from the nasal half of each retina cross at the optic chiasm and, together with uncrossed temporal-retina fibers, form the optic tracts; most of these fibers relay through the lateral geniculate body of the thalamus before reaching the visual cortex of the occipital lobe. p. 1981

As with Section 4, this book does not cover lobe functions (personality/decision-making for frontal, sensory processing specifics for parietal, etc.) or basal ganglia circuitry — that's neuroscience-course content, not gross anatomy.

6. The Cranial Nerves pp. 1976–2005

General Organization pp. 1976–1978

All 12 pairs are numbered rostral to caudal. Except for CN I and II (which are technically CNS extensions of the forebrain, not true peripheral nerves), the rest attach to nuclei in the brainstem. Cranial nerves can carry up to five different functional fiber types: somatic motor, visceral (parasympathetic) motor, somatic sensory, visceral sensory, and special sensory (smell, vision, hearing/balance, taste).

Pure motor nerves: III, IV, VI, XI, XII (plus the motor root of V).
Nerves carrying parasympathetic fibers: III, VII, IX, X.
Mixed nerves with both branchial motor and general sensory components: V, VII, IX, X (each associated with a different pharyngeal arch).

Testing tip carried over from your outline's own advice: have a study partner smile (VII), stick out their tongue (XII), and shrug their shoulders (XI) — tying each nerve to a physical action is far more durable than rote memorization of the list.

7. Blood Supply to the Brain pp. 1665–1673

The Two Arterial Sources p. 1665

Despite being only ~2.5% of body weight, the brain receives about one-sixth of cardiac output and one-fifth of resting oxygen consumption. Blood arrives via two paired vessels:

Internal Carotid Arteries — "Anterior Circulation" pp. 1666–1669

Arise from the common carotid arteries in the neck, enter the skull through the carotid canal, and pass through the cavernous sinus before terminating as the anterior and middle cerebral arteries.

Vertebral Arteries — "Posterior Circulation" p. 1669

Arise as the first branches of the subclavian arteries, ascend through the transverse foramina of the upper six cervical vertebrae, and enter the skull through the foramen magnum. The two vertebral arteries unite at the base of the pons to form the single basilar artery, which terminates by splitting into the paired posterior cerebral arteries.

The Cerebral Arterial Circle (Circle of Willis) p. 1670

A roughly pentagon-shaped anastomotic ring at the base of the brain, joining the anterior and posterior circulations and providing a built-in collateral pathway if one feeding vessel is compromised. Built front to back from: anterior communicating artery → anterior cerebral arteries → internal carotid arteries → posterior communicating arteries → posterior cerebral arteries. The circle surrounds the interpeduncular fossa on the underside of the midbrain.

Venous Drainage p. 1671

Thin-walled, valveless cerebral veins drain directly into the nearby dural venous sinuses (see Section 2). Superior cerebral veins go to the superior sagittal sinus; the deep great cerebral vein (of Galen) joins the inferior sagittal sinus to form the straight sinus.

Strokes — a sudden focal neurological deficit from disrupted cerebral blood flow, most often from an embolism blocking a major cerebral artery. Because the three cerebral arteries do anastomose with each other at the cortical surface, small emboli in peripheral branches can sometimes be compensated for — but occlusion of a major trunk overwhelms these microscopic connections, causing ischemia and infarction in that artery's territory. Knowing which artery = which territory is exactly how stroke localization works clinically (e.g., contralateral leg weakness suggests anterior cerebral artery territory; face/arm weakness plus language deficits suggests middle cerebral artery territory). p. 1673
The Circle of Willis is anatomically variable — one posterior cerebral artery arising as a branch of the internal carotid rather than the basilar system happens in roughly 1 in 3 people. These variants matter clinically because they change how well a person can tolerate occlusion of one of the main feeder vessels. p. 1673
